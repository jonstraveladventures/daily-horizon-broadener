For most of a thousand years, English law had a settled answer to the question of what to do when a thing killed a person: you took the thing. A cart that rolled over a sleeping drunk was forfeit. A horse a man fell from and drowned beneath was forfeit. A pig that savaged a child, a vat of scalding water, a millwheel, a well, a ship — each could be seized by the Crown once a coroner's jury judged it the cause of a death. The object was a *deodand*, from *deo dandum*, "to be given to God." In theory the king sold it and put the money to pious use, alms for the soul of the dead and the good of all his subjects. In practice the Crown collected a steady stream of other people's movable property.

The strange part is not that medieval people were superstitious about objects. They were oddly precise about them. The governing maxim held that "all things which, while in motion, cause death, are to be given to God," and juries took the motion clause to heart. When a cart crushed a child, they might rule that only the wheels had been moving and value the wheels alone, sparing the family the horses and the load. When a drunk sailor fell from a rope he had climbed, they forfeited the rope and left the ship untouched. This was casuistry, not animism: a working theory of blame that could be turned up into punishment or down into mercy, depending on whom the jury pitied.

And they did pity. The coroners' rolls are full of small kindnesses dressed as legal findings, a mill reduced to one unnamed part worth three shillings, a boy's death charged to the harbour rocks rather than the ship he fell from. One case has a Herefordshire couple, Robert and Leticia Russell, whose oxcart crushed their son Geoffrey. Rather than report it and lose the cart and oxen they lived by, they hid the boy's body in bed and prayed to Saint Thomas de Cantilupe. The miracle stories record that the boy came back to life. The cart, note, was saved too. The order of fear tells you everything: a grieving family's first dread was the forfeiture, not the coroner.

Then the machines arrived and the mercy dial jammed. Through the 1830s and 40s the railways killed people at industrial scale, and the companies met the bereaved with something close to indifference. Coroners' juries, angry and holding no other lever, reached for the oldest one to hand. They started declaring locomotives deodand. After a crash at Sonning Cutting on the Great Western in 1841, a jury dropped £1,100 of deodand on the engine and its wagons, a figure meant to bruise a corporation rather than fund a soul. A doctrine built to confiscate a peasant's horse was now pointed at the balance sheets of the richest firms in the country.

They did not stand for it. In 1846 Parliament abolished the deodand. In the same year it passed Lord Campbell's Act, which for the first time let a dead person's relatives sue the wrongdoer for money. The two laws are usually told as a single story of progress, out with the medieval relic, in with modern compensation. But look at what actually moved. We stopped blaming the object and started billing the owner. The thing that did the killing ceased to matter; the man who owned it, and the depth of his pockets, became the whole question. No philosophical awakening drove that. Railway owners refused to have their engines treated as guilty parties, and Parliament agreed.

Which leaves the older intuition unburied rather than answered. When something with no human at the controls does the killing, when the owner is a company and the choice belonged to no particular person, the deodand's question comes back wearing new clothes: do we blame the thing, or only ever the wallet standing behind it, and is that a difference we can still feel?
