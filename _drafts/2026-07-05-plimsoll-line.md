For much of the nineteenth century a certain kind of British ship went to sea half
expected to sink. They were called coffin ships: overloaded, badly repaired,
unseaworthy, and, in the detail that turns the stomach, often insured for more than
they were worth, so that an owner stood to gain more from a wreck than from a safe
arrival. A vessel worth more at the bottom of the sea than tied up at the quay is a
strange sort of asset, and the men aboard were the ones who made up the difference.
The sailors knew it. More than two thousand of them were taken to court for refusing
to board ships they judged unsafe, and in 1855 a group wrote to Queen Victoria to
protest that they were being convicted of desertion for declining to drown.

The man who made this his fight was Samuel Plimsoll, a coal merchant turned Member
of Parliament for Derby, who from 1870 pressed the case in the Commons and in 1873
put it before the public in a book, Our Seamen. He did not do it quietly; his
speeches were heated enough that shipowners took him to law over them. His wife Eliza
worked the cause alongside him as an equal partner. What they were asking for was
almost insultingly simple. Every merchant ship should carry a mark on its hull
showing how deep it could legally sit in the water. Load it past the mark, and it was
breaking the law rather than merely gambling with its crew.

That mark became one of the quietly familiar symbols of the sea: a circle a foot
across with a horizontal line drawn through its centre, painted amidships on each
side of the hull. The idea is elegant. A loaded ship settles lower in the water; when
the surface reaches the line, she is full. Anyone standing on the dock, with no
instruments and no arithmetic, can see at a glance whether a ship is over her limit.
In 1876 the Merchant Shipping Act made the line compulsory, and it looked like the
end of the coffin ship.

Except the Act contained a hole you could sail a fleet through. It made the line
compulsory but left it to the shipowner to decide where on the hull to paint it, and
to paint it himself. So some owners put their "maximum safe load" line exactly where
it suited them, high up the side, and a few, with a candour that is almost admirable,
painted it on the funnel, where the water could only reach it as the ship went down.
A mandatory safety limit, positioned by the very man it is meant to restrain, is not
a limit at all. It is a decoration.

Closing that hole took years. The Board of Trade only began enforcing the rules
Plimsoll had actually intended around 1890, and the position of the line was not
fixed in law until a further Merchant Shipping Act in 1894, nearly two decades after
the first. Only then did the mark reliably mean what everyone had assumed it meant
from the beginning.

The line is still there, on the hull of every cargo ship afloat, still called the
Plimsoll line, and it is rightly remembered as one of the great small victories of
safety regulation. But the part worth carrying away is those years in the middle,
when the rule existed and the number was left to the person it was written to
constrain. It is a lesson that seems to need relearning wherever a limit is set down:
a line is only as honest as whoever gets to decide where it goes, and the moment you
let the regulated draw their own, you have passed a law that protects no one and
looks very much like one that does…
