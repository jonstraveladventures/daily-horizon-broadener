A contract is normally one thing and its enforcement another. You sign a paper,
the words sit in a drawer, and somewhere behind them stands a court that will, if
it comes to it, send someone to make you keep your word. The paper remembers; the
state acts; the two are kept carefully apart. Among the Kongo peoples of Central
Africa there was an object that collapsed all of this into a single carved body,
and it did so in a way that still looks, from the outside, like a small miracle of
design.

The object is the nkisi nkondi, usually translated as a "power figure" and easier
to picture as a wooden man bristling with iron. What draws the eye is the surface:
dozens of nails, blades and pegs driven into the torso, the arms, the face, until
the figure seems to be suffering from the sheer density of hardware. Those nails
are not decoration and they are not an attack on the figure. Each one is an
agreement. When two parties came before the nkisi to settle a dispute, swear a
loyalty, or close a bargain, they sealed the moment by driving in a blade. Often
they first licked it, or fixed to it a scrap of their own hair or cloth or a little
saliva, so that a piece of the person went into the wood with the iron. From that
point the two were bound, and the figure held the bond in its body.

Now hold the consequence of that up to the light. A paper contract can be read by
anyone who finds it. This one cannot. The meaning of each nail lived only in the
memory of the nganga, the ritual specialist who kept the figure, and he carried the
entire ledger in his head: this peg, a minor matter now settled; that long blade
hammered deep, something graver, a killing perhaps. The figure was a filing system
whose index existed nowhere on the object itself. There is a detail that makes the
point almost unbearably concrete. If a vow was to be lifted, the nganga had to draw
out the correct nail, and only that one, because pulling the wrong nail released the
wrong oath. The archive could be edited, but a slip of the hand rewrote the law.

And the figure did not merely record. It enforced. Medicines were sealed into a
cavity in the belly or the head, often behind a fragment of mirror, and it was
these that gave the nkisi its charge, turning it from a passive register into
something that could act. Break the agreement whose nail you had driven, and the
nganga could rouse the figure to hunt the oath-breaker down with sickness or worse.
So the same wooden body was at once the document, the witness, the memory and the
bailiff. The nail you hammered in was your signature; it was also the thing that
would come for you.

There is a temptation to file this under superstition and move on, but that misses
what is genuinely clever about it. A society without writing still needs a way to
make promises stick across time, to store the fact of an agreement somewhere more
durable than talk and to attach a cost to breaking it. The nkisi does all three at
once, and it does something our own paper cannot: it makes the weight of the
community's unfinished business visible. You could look at the figure and see, in
the accumulating iron, how many quarrels and debts and loyalties the group was
carrying. A courthouse hides its caseload in cabinets. This one wore it on the
skin.

Many of these figures now stand disarmed in Western museums, taken during the
colonial carve-up of the Congo and cut off from the nganga who could read them, so
that what was once the most legible object in a community has become, to outsiders,
the most opaque. The Kongo peoples and their spiritual life are very much present;
it is this particular practice, and the memory each nail held, that has largely
gone. Which leaves the uncomfortable question of how much of any legal system is
really carried by the writing at all, and how much by a single person who simply
remembers what everything means.
