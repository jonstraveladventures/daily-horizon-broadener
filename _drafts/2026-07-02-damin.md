Imagine being handed an entire language in an afternoon. Not a phrasebook, not a
few polite formulas, but a working tongue you could use to say anything you might
say in your mother language. That is roughly what happened to young men of the
Lardil and Yangkaal peoples on Mornington Island, in the Gulf of Carpentaria,
when they reached the second stage of initiation. The senior men would take an
initiate aside and, field of meaning by field of meaning, call the words of Damin
at him while a second man answered back with the everyday Lardil equivalent.
Damin had somewhere between a hundred and fifty and two hundred root words. Lardil
has many thousands. And yet the initiate was expected to leave able to talk about
the same world.

The trick sits in that gap. If ordinary Lardil has a separate word for every kind
of fish, every relative, every act, and Damin has two hundred roots for the lot,
then each Damin word must do the work of dozens. The way it manages this is the
part that should stop you. Damin does not simply have fewer, vaguer words. It
appears to break meaning into a small stock of general concepts and rebuild the
specific from them by combination. A wooden axe, for instance, is not given its
own name; it is assembled as something close to "wood that affects honey" — the
tool named by what it does to the tree and the hive rather than by a label you
would have to be told. Where Lardil has nineteen or so pronouns, sorted by number
and kinship and who stands in what relation to whom, Damin has two: *n!aa*, "me",
and *n!uu*, "not me". Ego and everything else. Hale worked out that the kinship
system collapses the same way, the whole tangle of Lardil relations folded down
into a handful of logical primitives from which the rest can be derived. You do
not memorise a new word for each cousin; you compute the cousin.

The linguist Ken Hale, who spent much of his life among Australian languages and
picked them up with a speed that unsettled people, recorded Damin in the 1960s and
70s from among the last men who still had it, and never quite got over it. What
struck him was not the exotica but the design. Here was an oral culture, with no
writing and no grammarians and no dictionaries, that had produced something a
philosopher would recognise on sight: an attempt to find the smallest set of
primitive ideas out of which all the others can be composed. Leibniz dreamed of
exactly this, an alphabet of human thought, and never built it. Decades later the
linguist Anna Wierzbicka would spend a career arguing that every language rests on
a few dozen indefinable "semantic primes," and Damin keeps turning up in that
debate as the awkward, thrilling piece of evidence — a real community that seems
to have run the analysis for itself, for reasons that had nothing to do with
logic and everything to do with the sacred.

Then there is the sound of the thing, and this is where it stops being an abstract
puzzle and becomes physical. Everyday Lardil, like nearly every Australian
language, is made entirely on the outward breath. Damin keeps some of those
consonants and then adds four more ways of moving air that appear nowhere else on
the continent: nasal clicks, made by sucking the tongue off the roof of the mouth,
the only clicks known outside southern Africa; a velar ejective, punched out with
the throat closed; a lateral fricative drawn inward, a sound made on the breath
going the wrong way; and a bilabial "spurt" of air from the lips. A boy already
fluent in Lardil would have found Damin not merely strange in its vocabulary but
alien in the mouth, marked at every syllable as a different order of speech. That
seems to be the point. This was not a shorthand for gossip. It was language made
deliberately other, reserved for men who had passed through a particular door, and
in the culture's own telling it was not invented by anyone but sung into being by
an ancestral figure at the beginning of things.

The last full initiation that would have passed Damin on was held in the 1950s. The
mission discouraged the old rites, the chain of teaching broke, and within a couple
of decades there was almost no one left who could speak it. So we are left with a
small, deliberate, astonishing invention, written down just in time and now
essentially gone: proof that the impulse to strip meaning to the bone and start
again is not the private property of logicians and code-breakers, but something a
community can do on its own, in secret, for the sake of the holy rather than the
efficient. What else was worked out like this, in languages no one wrote down, and
lost before anyone thought to ask how it had been built?
