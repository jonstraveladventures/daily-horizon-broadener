There is a particular kind of idea that I find genuinely unsettling, the kind that
takes something I had filed under logic and mathematics and shows it turning up,
fully worked out, in a place with no writing and no universities and no interest
in either. Damin is one of those. It was a secret language, spoken only by the
initiated men of the Lardil and Yangkaal peoples on Mornington Island, in the Gulf
of Carpentaria, and the thing that should stop you is not that it was secret but
that it was so small. Somewhere between a hundred and fifty and two hundred words,
against the many thousands of everyday Lardil, and yet a young man was meant to
walk out of the ceremony able to say in it anything he could say in his mother
tongue.

Sit with the arithmetic of that for a moment. If ordinary Lardil has a separate
word for every fish and every cousin and every kind of act, and Damin has two
hundred words for the lot, then each Damin word has to carry the load of dozens.
The interesting part is how it does the carrying. Damin does not simply go vague
and hope you fill in the rest. It seems to break meaning down into a small stock of
very general ideas and then build the specific back up out of them, so that a
wooden axe is not handed its own name but assembled as something close to "wood
that affects honey", the tool described by what it does to the tree and the hive.
Where everyday Lardil has nineteen or so pronouns, sorted by number and kinship and
who is related to whom, Damin has two, one meaning "me" and one meaning "not me".
Ego, and everything else. Ken Hale, the linguist who recorded it and who could pick
up languages at a speed that unnerved people, worked out that the kinship system
folds down the same way, the whole tangle of relations reduced to a few logical
primitives you compute rather than memorise. You do not learn a word for the
cousin. You work the cousin out.

Now, I spend my days around people who dream of exactly this, the reduction of a
messy world to a short list of primitives from which the rest follows, and it is
faintly humbling to find that a community on an island did it first and did it for
reasons that had nothing to do with tidiness. Leibniz wanted an alphabet of human
thought and never built it. The linguist Anna Wierzbicka has spent a career arguing
that every language rests on a few dozen indefinable "semantic primes", and Damin
keeps turning up in that argument as the awkward, thrilling piece of evidence, a
real people who appear to have run the analysis themselves, not for philosophy but
for the sacred.

And then there is the sound of it, which is where the whole thing stops being an
abstract puzzle and becomes something you feel in the mouth. Everyday Lardil, like
almost every Australian language, is made entirely on the outward breath. Damin
keeps some of those consonants and then adds four other ways of moving air that
appear nowhere else on the continent, among them clicks of the kind otherwise known
only from southern Africa, an ejective punched out with the throat shut, and a
sound drawn inward on the breath going the wrong way. A boy already fluent in
Lardil would have found Damin not just strange in its words but physically foreign
at every syllable, marked at each breath as a different order of speech. That seems
to be the point of it. This was never a shorthand for gossip. It was language made
deliberately other, reserved for men who had passed through a particular door, and
in the telling it was not invented by anyone at all but sung into being by an
ancestral figure at the start of things.

The last full initiation that would have handed Damin on was held in the 1950s. The
mission discouraged the old rites, the chain of teaching broke, and within a couple
of decades there was almost nobody left who could speak it. So we are left with a
small, deliberate, astonishing thing, written down just in time and now essentially
gone, and with the uncomfortable knowledge that the urge to strip meaning to the
bone and start again is not the private property of logicians after all. What else
was worked out like this, in languages nobody wrote down, and lost before anyone
thought to ask how it had been built…
