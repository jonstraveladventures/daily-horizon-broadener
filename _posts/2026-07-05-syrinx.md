Listen to a wood thrush at dusk and you seem to be hearing two birds, one phrase
braided against another, in a harmony too exact for two separate singers to keep. But
there is only one bird. It is singing both parts itself, in the same instant, because
unlike you it has two voices and can work them independently. The organ that lets it
do this has no equivalent in the human body, and once you know it is there, birdsong
stops being background and becomes a small feat of engineering going on in every
hedge.

The organ is the syrinx, and the first surprise is where it sits. Your own voice comes
from the larynx, high in the throat, a single valve the breath passes through on its
way out. A bird's sound source is much lower down, at the very base of the windpipe,
right at the point where the trachea forks into the two tubes running to the lungs.
That fork is the whole trick. Because the syrinx straddles a junction, it is not one
instrument but two, one seated on each branch, and a songbird runs separate muscles
and nerves to each side, so the two can be controlled apart from one another.

There is a second surprise in how the sound itself is made. Humans, like other
mammals, vibrate vocal folds, flaps of tissue in that single throat valve. A bird has
no vocal cords at all. Instead, thin membranes in the walls of the syrinx are set
trembling by the air rushing past them, and that trembling is the note. With one such
membrane on each branch of the fork, and independent control over each, the bird can
tension and release the two sides separately. In effect it is playing two wind
instruments at once, with a single set of lungs feeding both.

This is why some songbirds can do what no human voice can. The wood thrush's
flute-like "ee-o-lay" is, as the people who study it put it, a one-bird duet: it has
the equivalent of two sets of vocal cords and sings two overlapping songs together.
The veery goes further still, and at one point in its spiralling song it sends one
melody climbing while the other descends, both from the same small throat on the same
breath. We cannot come near this. To make a chord, humans have to cooperate, one voice
each. The closest a single person gets is Tuvan throat singing, and even that is one
melodic line sounded together with its own overtones, not two lines walking in
different directions.

So the ordinary birdsong outside the window is stranger than it sounds, and the
strangeness is not in the tune but in the fact that a small animal can hold two
independent musical lines in its throat and voice them at once. It leaves a question
that is oddly hard to grip from inside a single-voiced body. When the bird sings its
two parts, is it doing one difficult thing, or two easy things at the same time, and
is there any way for us, who can only ever mean one note at a time, to tell which it
is…
