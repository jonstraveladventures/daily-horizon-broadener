A coral has a problem that sounds impossible to solve. It is an animal, but it is
cemented to the spot, and it will never in its life move closer to another of its
kind. It cannot court, cannot chase, cannot even turn to face a mate. And yet it
has to breed. The solution the reef has arrived at is one of the strangest and
largest coordinated events anywhere in nature, and for most of scientific history
nobody knew it happened at all.

The answer is to give up on meeting altogether and let the sea be the meeting
place. On one night a year, the corals of the Great Barrier Reef release their eggs
and sperm into the water all at the same time, in such quantity that the ocean turns
cloudy and the surface fills with slicks of pink and orange. More than a hundred and
thirty species of stony coral take part, along with hundreds of other creatures, and
they do it within a window of a night or two, along hundreds of kilometres of reef.
Each colony spills its cells into the current, and fertilisation happens out there,
in open water, in the dark. For an animal that cannot move, timing is the whole of
courtship. If you are a night early or a night late, you have simply failed.

Which raises the obvious question: how does a reef agree on the night? A coral has
no eyes and no brain, only a thin sheet of near-identical polyps. It has no way of
counting down to a date, and no way of asking its neighbours what they intend to do.
And yet the synchrony is exact enough to fill a stretch of ocean the size of a
country on cue. The mechanism, as far as it has been worked out, is a stack of
signals read in sequence. The season sets the stage through water temperature, which
has to climb to around twenty-seven or twenty-eight degrees. The month is set by the
full moon. And the final trigger, the thing that says tonight and not tomorrow, is
the moon itself, or rather the particular way its light dims in the nights after it
is full. Corals carry light-sensing molecules, the same family that helps other
creatures keep time, and with them a brainless animal effectively reads the phase of
the moon off the surface of the sea and acts on it.

Sit with that for a moment. There is no signal passed from colony to colony, no
chorus starting on one reef and spreading to the next. Every coral is reading the
same sky and the same water independently, and arriving at the same answer, which is
why they all move together without anything you could call communication. The
coordination is real but there is no coordinator. It is the tide of shared
conditions doing the work that, in almost any other animal, courtship would have to
do.

The strangest part may be how recently anyone noticed. This is one of the biggest
synchronised events on the planet, happening every year on the most studied reef in
the world, in shallow water, in plain sight, and it was only described by science in
the early 1980s. Divers had seen fragments of it; no one had put together that the
whole reef was doing one thing on one night. For a very long time a spectacle on this
scale simply fell through the gap between what people happened to be looking at and
when they happened to be looking.

So the reef keeps an appointment that nothing on it can remember making, set by a
clock none of its members can read the way we would, and it keeps that appointment
so precisely that the sea changes colour. It makes you wonder what else runs on a
schedule like that, large and old and exact, waiting on the one night a year someone
finally happens to be in the water to see it…
