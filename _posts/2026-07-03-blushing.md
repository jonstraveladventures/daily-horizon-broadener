Charles Darwin thought blushing was the hardest thing his theory of evolution had
to account for. He called it "the most peculiar and the most human of all
expressions," and the difficulty was not that it was human but that it seemed to
work against the person doing it. As far as anyone can tell we are the only
creatures that do it; no other animal floods its face with blood when it is caught
out. And the blush arrives at exactly the wrong moment. Caught in a small lie,
singled out for praise, made suddenly aware that people are looking at you, your
face reddens and broadcasts to the whole room the one thing you were trying to keep
off it. Natural selection is supposed to favour traits that help. Here is one that
looks designed to give you away.

The mechanics only sharpen the problem. A blush is not something you can decide to
do, the way you can decide to smile, and it is not something you can decide to stop.
The harder you try to suppress it, the hotter it climbs, which is why being told
"you're going red" is a guarantee of going redder. Fear does the opposite to the
face, draining the blood and leaving it pale; embarrassment drives the blood
upward, and only into the face, ears and neck, the parts of you that another person
reads first. Whatever this is, the body has wired it to be seen, and wired it beyond
the reach of the will. If you were designing a creature to survive by hiding its
weaknesses, the blush is close to the last feature you would add.

The resolution, worked out long after Darwin, is to turn the paradox over and read
it the other way round. The blush is valuable because it cannot be controlled.
Picture the small, interdependent groups our ancestors lived in, where the same few
dozen people fed you, defended you and could, if they chose, cast you out, and where
a broken trust had to be repaired or you were in serious trouble. In that world an
honest, unfakeable sign that you knew you had done wrong was worth a great deal.
Anyone can say sorry, and everyone knows how little the words cost. A blush cannot
be summoned to order and cannot be swallowed, so it cannot be faked, and that is
precisely what gives it weight. It is a confession the body makes on your behalf
before you have decided whether to make it. Experiments bear this out: people who
visibly redden after a blunder are judged more trustworthy, and are forgiven more
readily, than those who stay composed. The thing that mortifies you is quietly
buying back your place in the group.

So the involuntary betrayal is not a flaw in the system, it is the system. The
signal works as a signal only because you had no say in sending it, and the moment
you could control it, it would be worth nothing, because everyone would know it
could be staged. Evolution seems to have solved the problem of proving you mean it
by taking the choice away from you altogether. Sincerity, on this account, is not
something you perform. It is something that happens to you.

Which leaves an uncomfortable thread hanging. We admire self-possession and train
ourselves out of showing too much, yet the most credible thing we can communicate
is the one thing we cannot govern at all. And more and more of what we say now
passes through text and screens, where no face changes colour and no one can see the
tell, so that we are slowly retiring the one guarantee of honesty we never had to
choose to give. It is worth wondering what fills the gap, once the blush has nowhere
left to show…
