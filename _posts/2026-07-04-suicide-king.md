In every standard deck of cards there is a small act of violence that nobody
planned. The King of Hearts holds a sword whose blade vanishes behind his head,
angled so that it looks, unmistakably, as though he is driving it into his own
skull. Card players call him the suicide king. The strange part is that no artist
ever decided he should die this way. The blade is a mistake, and the mistake is
about four hundred years old.

Trace the card backwards and the weapon changes in your hands. The English court
cards descend from a pattern cut in Rouen, a French city that exported playing
cards by the crate in the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries. On those early cards
the King of Hearts is not stabbing anyone. He is holding a battle-axe, raised
beside his head the way a king on a card conventionally holds a weapon, blade
outward, entirely at ease. Somewhere between that king and the one in your pocket,
the axe became a sword and the sword turned inward.

The reason is the way cards were made. They were printed from woodblocks, and the
blockmakers did not work from the original. They copied the most recent batch of
cards, which had themselves been copied from an earlier batch, which had been
copied from one before that. Each new block was cut by eye from a worn, ink-clogged
print of its predecessor, and each time a little detail fell away. The fine lines
around the king's neck and hair blurred and dropped out. The head of the axe, a
small and fiddly shape, was among the first things to vanish. What survived was the
shaft, a plain straight line, and once the detail around it was gone the eye read
that line as a sword and read its point, emerging near the crown, as going in.
Nobody chose this. It accumulated, copy by copy, until by about 1800 the axe was
unmistakably a blade and the king unmistakably stabbing himself.

That is what makes the card quietly remarkable. It is a fossil. The same thing that
happens to a sentence whispered around a room, or to a manuscript recopied by tired
scribes, or to a gene copied down the generations, happened here to a picture, in
public, on an object that ended up in almost every house on earth. The King of
Hearts is not a design. He is the frozen output of a long chain of small errors,
each one faithfully preserved by the next person in the chain, none of whom went
back to check the source. He is also the only king in the deck with no moustache,
and for the same reason: somewhere a line was lost and never put back.

Here is the part that should nag. Nobody corrected the sword because, by the time
the deck was standardised, nobody knew there was anything to correct. The shape had
become the tradition. Cardmakers reproduced the suicide king exactly because that
was simply what a King of Hearts looked like; the axe, and the intention behind it,
had been forgotten so completely that the error was now the rule. When printing
finally froze the English court cards into their modern form, it froze a mistake
and stamped it onto every deck that came after.

Which raises a question you can carry to almost any familiar thing. We tend to
assume that whatever we have copied for centuries looks the way it does because
someone meant it to. The King of Hearts is proof that a shape can persist for four
hundred years, be reproduced billions of times, and mean nothing at all beyond the
fact that somebody, long ago, copied it slightly wrong, and no one after them ever
looked back. How much of what we take for settled tradition is not design but
drift, held in place only because the original is too far gone to miss…
